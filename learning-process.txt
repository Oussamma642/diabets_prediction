The easiest way to understand the difference between these four variables is to think about **how a student studies for a final exam**. 

In supervised machine learning, your algorithm is the student. 

Before we break down the four variables, let's look at the two main letters:
* **$X$ (The Features):** These are the **Questions** on the exam. In your dataset, this is the patient's data (Glucose, BMI, Blood Pressure, etc.).
* **$Y$ (The Labels):** This is the **Answer Key**. In your dataset, this is the `Outcome` column (1 for Diabetic, 0 for Healthy).

When you use `train_test_split`, you are creating a study guide and a final exam for your algorithm. Here is exactly what each piece does:



### 1. The Study Guide (Training Set - 80%)
This is the material you give to the algorithm so it can learn. 

* **`X_train` (The Practice Questions):** You hand the algorithm 80% of your patients' medical data. It looks at the BMI, the Glucose, etc., and tries to find patterns.
* **`Y_train` (The Practice Answer Key):** You *also* give the algorithm the correct diagnoses for those specific patients. The algorithm makes a guess using `X_train`, checks the real answer in `Y_train`, and adjusts its internal math until it gets it right. **This is where the actual "learning" happens.**

### 2. The Final Exam (Testing Set - 20%)
Once the algorithm says, "I'm ready, I've learned the patterns," you take the study guide away and give it the final exam to see how smart it *really* is.

* **`X_test` (The Final Exam Questions):** You hand the algorithm the remaining 20% of the patients' medical data. **Crucially, the algorithm has never seen these patients before.** It must use the math it learned during training to predict whether each of these new patients has diabetes.
* **`Y_test` (The Teacher's Answer Key):** The algorithm **never** gets to see this! This is the true diagnosis for those 20% of patients, and it is strictly for *you* (the data scientist). After the algorithm makes its predictions, you compare its guesses to `Y_test` to calculate its final grade (the accuracy percentage). 

### Summary in a Single Sentence:
The algorithm uses **`X_train`** and **`Y_train`** to learn how to diagnose diabetes, and then it looks at the unseen patients in **`X_test`** to make predictions, which you grade by comparing them to the hidden truth in **`Y_test`**.
